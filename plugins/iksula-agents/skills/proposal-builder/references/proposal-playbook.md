# Iksula Proposal Playbook

*CEO-Focused Proposals - Universal Build Instructions, Structure & Format Spec*

*Read this before building any client proposal. v1 · 03 Jun 2026*

## 0. How to Use This Document

This is the single brief you give Claude every time you want a client proposal built. It defines three things, in priority order:

- What it locks: the format to use (the visual/structural rules - locked, never reinvented);
- What it shapes: the storyline and section structure a CEO expects to read;
- What it specifies: for each section, the exact items to cover and the business question that section must answer.

Working method when building a new proposal:

1. Copy, don’t recreate. Start from the approved reference file (see §3.1) - copy it, do not rebuild from scratch. The format, cover, TOC, header/footer and boilerplate sections come for free and must not be touched (see §4).
2. Edit the narrative. Rewrite only the client-specific sections (Executive Summary, Our Understanding, Approach specifics, Reuse/Migration/Risk specifics, Scope, client-provided Assumptions, Exclusions, and case-study selection).
3. Respect the lean-body rule. Keep the body lean and push all depth to the Appendix (see §5 and §6).
4. Verify. Run the CEO decision checklist (§7) and the pre-flight checklist (§8) before declaring it done.

## 1. The CEO Lens - Why and How

A CEO does not read a proposal end-to-end like a spec. They skim for the answer to one question - “should I trust these people with this, and what does it cost me?” - and they decide fast. Every rule below exists to serve that reading behaviour.

- One storyline, not sections. The proposal must read as one continuous argument, not a stack of disconnected boxes. The spine: here is your situation → here is why we are the right partner → here is what we understand → here is how we deliver it fast (and where AI helps) → here is how we keep your live platform safe → here is what it costs.
- Lean body, deep appendix. The body is scannable in a few minutes - short paragraphs, bullets, bold lead-ins, a clear outcome plus a pointer. Anyone who wants the detail follows the pointer into the Appendix. Nothing essential is buried; nothing long sits in the body.
- Reach commercials fast. A CEO wants “what, how fast, how much” early. Do not make them travel through twelve sections to find the number. Keep the path to Commercials short.
- Lead with the differentiator. Open with the single thing that wins the deal for this client (speed, price, a proven analogous build, an AI efficiency). State it plainly in the Executive Summary.
- Decision-first. Every section earns its place by answering a real business question (see the checklist in §7). If a section answers nothing the CEO is asking, it belongs in the Appendix or nowhere.
- Honest over impressive. Realistic beats salesy. Credible numbers, honest ownership of risk, and clear boundaries build more trust with a CEO than superlatives. If we win on TAT and price, we lead with believable efficiency - not hype.

## 2. Hard Language Rules

These are not preferences - they are constraints. Apply them to every line.

- Plain, positive, professional. Write the way a competent person speaks to a busy executive.
- No buzzwords, no catchy or tacky lines, no marketing filler. Simple, direct headings (“Our Approach for Delivery”, “Risks and Mitigation”).
- No negatives and no commentary on any other vendor - anywhere, including case studies. Reframe past work as our work only; never characterise an incumbent or competitor.
- Body text is bullets and short paragraphs with bold lead-ins. No walls of prose.
- Each body point gives a short outcome and a pointer to the Appendix for detail - it does not contain the detail.
- Numbers are credible and consistent across the document (the effort, timeline and commercial figures must agree).

## 3. Format Specification (Lock & Build)

The format is already correct in the reference file. Do not redesign it. This section documents it so it can be reproduced and verified, not changed.

### 3.1 Reference file

Always build a new proposal by copying the latest approved instance of this format:

- Prefer the latest **approved proposal instance in `_spine/`** when one exists.
- Otherwise copy the bundled clean template `assets/Iksula_Proposal_TEMPLATE_v1_19Jun2026.docx`.

It is 100% aligned to the format guidelines (fonts, sizes, colours, borders, table colours, spacing). Copying it preserves all of that natively.

### 3.2 Visual identity - warm blue

The Iksula design palette is warm blue. Use these exact values; do not substitute.

| Role | Hex | Where it is used |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Primary / warm blue | 4A66AC | Heading 1 bar (white text on this fill); table header cells; cover title |
| Dark navy (text) | 243255 | Heading 2 / Heading 3 text; bold lead-ins; table body text |
| Light fill | D9DFEF | Heading 2 / Heading 3 bar fill |

### 3.3 Page & type

- Page: US Letter (8.5″ × 11″), portrait, ~0.9″ margins all sides.
- Font: Poppins throughout. Body (Normal) 10pt.

### 3.4 Headings

- Heading 1: 13pt bold, white text on a warm-blue 4A66AC bar. Manual numbering (“1. ”, “2. ”). The bar comes from the style - applying Heading 1 gives it.
- Heading 2 / 3: 11pt bold, navy 243255 text on a light D9DFEF bar. Numbered as sub-sections (1.1, 1.2 …).

### 3.5 Tables

- Use the Table Grid style. Header row cells filled warm-blue 4A66AC with white bold text (~7.5-8pt); body text ~7-8.5pt in navy.
- Tables are the default way to present comparisons, classifications, RACI, risk registers and reuse scenarios - they scan faster than prose.

### 3.6 Cover, table of contents, header / footer, border

- Structure: The front matter is two content controls: [0] the cover page and [1] a live Table of Contents field.
- Chrome: The page border, header and footer (page number) are part of the format.
- Rule: None of the above is regenerated. It is kept exactly as in the reference file; the user edits cover text manually and refreshes the TOC field (F9) in Word.

## 5. Proposal Structure - Body (Section by Section)

This is the body order. For each section: its purpose, the CEO question it answers, the key items to cover, and whether detail lives here or in the Appendix. Sub-numbering matches the reference file.

Executive Summary

Purpose: Win the read in the first half page - situation, why us, what we will do, and the headline differentiator.

CEO question it answers: Can I trust these people with this, and roughly what am I signing up for?

Key items to cover:

- The client’s situation in 2-3 lines (their goal, where they are now).
- Why Iksula is the right partner - the single strongest proof point for this client.
- What we will deliver, in one line, and the headline timeline.
- The differentiator that wins the deal (e.g. speed + price, a proven analogous build, AI-accelerated delivery).

What drives the business decision: Trust + the differentiator. This is the only section guaranteed to be read.

Body vs appendix: All in body. No detail - pointers only.

## 1. About Iksula

Purpose: Establish credibility quickly with relevant, comparable experience - not a company brochure.

CEO question it answers: Have they done something genuinely like this before?

Key items to cover:

- A short capability statement (kept as-is boilerplate - see §4).
- A relevant-experience sub-section (1.1) framed to the client’s problem type (e.g. CMS / headless / multi-brand).
- A client-logo strip if available.

What drives the business decision: Comparable proof reduces perceived risk.

Body vs appendix: Headline experience in body; full case studies in Appendix B.

## 2. Our Understanding

Purpose: Show we understand the client’s real situation - especially that it is a live, shared or constrained environment, not a clean slate.

CEO question it answers: Do they actually get my situation and its constraints?

Key items to cover:

- The current state in plain terms (what exists, what is live, what is shared).
- The specific job to be done and its boundaries.
- The constraints that shape delivery (live traffic, shared backend, existing partner, benchmarks).

What drives the business decision: Demonstrated understanding is the basis for trusting the plan that follows.

Body vs appendix: Concise framing in body; any inventory or detailed mapping in the Appendix.

## 3. Our Approach for Delivery

Purpose: Show how the work gets done, fast and safely - this is the CEO’s lead beat when speed/efficiency wins the deal.

CEO question it answers: How will they deliver, how fast, and how do they go that fast credibly?

Key items to cover:

- 3.1 Delivery phases - the path from discovery to go-live, with the headline timeline.
- 3.2 Use of AI in delivery - realistic, activity-level leverage (where AI helps, what stays manual). Honest percentages, not hype.

What drives the business decision: Speed + credible method. If we win on TAT, this is where it is proven.

Body vs appendix: Phase summary + AI summary in body; per-activity AI table and detailed delivery plan in Appendices C and D.

## 4. Reuse, Migration and Risk Management

Purpose: Reassure the CEO that working on a live / shared platform is safe and that ownership is clear.

CEO question it answers: What is the risk to my live platform, and who owns it when something breaks?

Key items to cover:

- 4.1 What we reuse - the reuse/new/retest stance.
- 4.2 How the migration runs - the safe-cutover approach.
- 4.3 Reused code and ownership - we own what we touch; we fix breakage we cause, never redirect.
- 4.4 Working with the existing platform partner - the cooperation model (no commentary on the partner).
- 4.5 What we need from the client - dependencies and access.
- 4.6 Risks and mitigation - the top risks and how each is handled.

What drives the business decision: Risk containment + clear ownership. This is what makes a CEO comfortable saying yes on a live system.

Body vs appendix: Short stance per sub-section in body; reuse classification, RACI, reuse scenarios and full risk register in Appendices E-H.

## 5. Scope of Work

Purpose: State the scope at a glance and point to the authoritative detailed scope.

CEO question it answers: What exactly am I buying?

Key items to cover:

- A one-paragraph scope summary.
- A pointer to Appendix A for the full, detailed scope of work.

What drives the business decision: Clear boundaries prevent later disputes.

Body vs appendix: Summary only in body; the full functional scope table in Appendix A.

## 6. Commercials

Purpose: Give the price clearly, reached quickly. (Kept-as-is section - see §4; only the deal figures change.)

CEO question it answers: How much, and on what basis?

Key items to cover:

- The commercial figure(s) and what they cover.
- The basis (effort, team, duration) consistent with the approach and scope.

What drives the business decision: The number, and whether it is justified by everything above it.

Body vs appendix: All in body.

## 7. Payment Terms

Purpose: State the payment schedule and terms. (Kept-as-is section - see §4.)

CEO question it answers: When do I pay, and against what?

Key items to cover:

- The milestone / schedule and any commercial terms.

What drives the business decision: Cash-flow and commitment shape.

Body vs appendix: All in body.

## 8. Support and AMS

Purpose: Describe ongoing support after go-live. (Kept-as-is section - do not change a single word; see §4.)

CEO question it answers: What happens after launch, and who keeps it running?

Key items to cover:

- 8.1 BAU task scope.
- 8.2 Engagement structure and terms.

What drives the business decision: Continuity and long-term ownership.

Body vs appendix: All in body.

## 9. Assumptions

Purpose: Make dependencies and conditions explicit so the commercial holds.

CEO question it answers: What am I responsible for, and what is this price conditional on?

Key items to cover:

- 9.1 To be provided by the client - access, content, environments, decisions.
- 9.2 Reuse and migration assumptions.
- 9.3 Delivery assumptions (kept-as-is sub-section - see §4).

What drives the business decision: Conditions that protect the timeline and price; client obligations.

Body vs appendix: All in body - assumptions are deliverable-related and kept detailed.

## 10. Exclusions

Purpose: State plainly what is not included, to bound the engagement.

CEO question it answers: What is NOT covered, so there are no surprises?

Key items to cover:

- A clear list of out-of-scope items.

What drives the business decision: Boundary clarity; protects both sides.

Body vs appendix: All in body.

## 11. Change Control

Purpose: Define how changes to scope are handled commercially.

CEO question it answers: If I want more or things change, how is that handled?

Key items to cover:

- The change-request process and how effort/cost is treated.
- The link to ownership of fixes (pre-existing defects via CR; introduced defects at our cost).

What drives the business decision: Predictability when scope shifts.

Body vs appendix: All in body - kept detailed.

## 7. The CEO Decision Checklist

Before the proposal is done, confirm each of these questions is answered clearly, in the right place. If any is missing or buried, fix it.

| # | The question a CEO asks | Answered in |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Can they actually do this? | About Iksula + Appendix B (case studies) |
| 2 | Do they understand my situation and constraints? | 2. Our Understanding |
| 3 | How will they deliver, and how fast? | 3. Approach (phases + AI) |
| 4 | What is the risk to my live platform, and who owns fixes? | 4. Reuse / Migration / Risk |
| 5 | What exactly am I buying? | 5. Scope + Appendix A |
| 6 | How much does it cost, and when do I pay? | 6. Commercials + 7. Payment |
| 7 | What do I owe, what’s excluded, and how are changes handled? | 9. Assumptions, 10. Exclusions, 11. Change Control |

## 8. Pre-Flight Checklist Before Sending

- Built by copying the reference file; format, cover, TOC, header/footer, border untouched (§3, §4).
- Keep-as-is sections (About Iksula, Commercials, Payment, Support/AMS, Delivery Assumptions) unchanged except deal figures (§4).
- Body is lean and scannable; all depth is in the Appendix (§5, §6).
- Executive Summary leads with the differentiator and reaches the point fast (§1).
- Language rules obeyed - no buzzwords, no negatives, no vendor commentary (§2).
- All seven CEO questions answered clearly and in the right place (§7).
- Effort, timeline and commercial numbers are consistent across the document.
- Appendix A scope is the authoritative scope; body §5 points to it.
- Cover text edited and TOC field refreshed (F9) by the user in Word.
